Capital to Total Assets:

  • Capital/Total Assets
  • It serves as a gauge of the deposit-taking sector's capital adequacy and shows the percentage of assets that are funded by sources other than own funds.

Regulatory Capital to Risk-Weighted Assets:

  • Regulatory capital/risk-weighted assets
  • It assesses the capital adequacy of deposit takers ie the quality of capital available to financial organizations. This determines how well they can withstand shocks to their balance sheets.

Unfortunately, the Great Recession of 2008–2009 and the ensuing European debt crisis affected many wealthy countries. Italy was hit the hardest and the national economy contracted by 6.76%, marking seven quarters of recession. (http://stats.oecd.org/index.aspx?queryid=350). Italy's credit rating dropped in 2011 as a result of the Silvio Berlusconi administration's continued instability, which raised concerns about the nation's public debt. The evolution of Italy's return on assets, return on equity & gross liability position in financial derivatives to capital reflect the story above. Return on assets "is calculated by dividing net income before extraordinary items and taxes by the average value of total assets " and return on equity "is calculated by dividing net income before extraordinary items and taxes by the average value of capital". The former measures deposit takers’ "efficiency in using their assets" and the latter, the "efficiency in using their capital". As expected, both these metrics fell during the recessions (see the graphs below) and the double dip can even be seen! Gross liability position in financial derivatives to capital " is calculated by using the market value of financial derivative liabilities as the numerator and capital as the denominator". It shows the "exposure of deposit takers’ financial derivative liability positions relative to capital." In line with the tighter restrictions and new policies, this metric dropped over the course of the period studied (see the charts below).

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau explains that "Credit cards are a fundamental component of consumer credit access and financial resilience; understanding the impact of CLDs (Credit line decreases) on consumers can help inform policymakers and market participants on the importance of these credit limit account management decisions." As such, I chose to explore how consumer credit is affected by recessions.

Revolving and nonrevolving credit make up the two main categories of total consumer credit. Until the lender or borrower ends the account, revolving credit is active. A non-revolving line of credit, on the other hand, is a one-time arrangement, and the lender shuts the account after the credit line is repaid. The most widely used type of revolving credit is credit cards. Consumer auto and student loan debt compose the majority of nonrevolving credit.

Since 2000 total consumer credit has increased (1st graph). However, we see that the growth turns slightly negative during periods of recession, namely during the housing crisis in 2007 and during the Covid crisis in 2020. There is more revolving than non-revolving credit. When we zoom in on the percent change in consumer credit we can see these dips in more detail (graph 2). Interestingly, revolving credit sees larger swings in percent change. Moreover, revolving credit saw a steeper negative percent change during the recent pandemic than during the housing crisis while non-revolving credit saw a steeper decline during the housing crisis. Perhaps this can be explained by the nature of the crisis. The house in crisis affected the lines of credit associated with non-revolving credit whereas the pandemic more affected consumer behavior and the types of credit typically associated with credit cards. Further, revolving credit saw an outsized recovery since the pandemic despite inflationary pressures.

The negative effects of recessions on credit lines are in no way shocking. Households may be less likely to pay back their debt because of the significant unemployment and income shocks brought on by recessions. Financial institutions, therefore, restrict access to credit. This is substantiated by the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. They explain that "Credit card account management strategies can vary significantly by issuer. In the event of a broad economic downturn, issuers might reduce credit limits to prevent incurring losses from consumers they believe will be unable to repay. For example, CLD activity increased during the COVID-19 pandemic and the Great Recession." (https://files.consumerfinance.gov/f/documents/cfpb_credit-card-line-decreases_report_2022-06.pdf). In fact, FICO is highly cognisant of this tension and reported that "The COVID-19 Pandemic and ensuing financial strain on millions of customers is a top of mind concern for risk managers these days. With recently originated loans showing signs of deterioration in repayment odds." (https://www.fico.com/en/latest-thinking/webinar/north-america-what-fico-score-dynamics-prior-downturns-and-natural)
